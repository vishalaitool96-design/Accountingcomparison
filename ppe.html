SECTION A: Core Technical Comparison Table
Parameter AS 10 (Old Indian GAAP) Ind AS 16 IAS 16 (IFRS) US GAAP ASC 360 Logic shift
Core philosophy Focuses on historical cost with limited revaluation use. Cost model or revaluation model, with component accounting. Same broad model as Ind AS. Mostly historical cost, with some limited fair value options. From simple cost tracking to more judgment-based asset measurement.
Initial recognition PPE recognized when future economic benefits are probable and cost can be measured reliably. Same. Same. Same. Recognition rules are broadly aligned.
Initial measurement At cost, including purchase price, non-refundable taxes, freight, installation, and directly attributable costs. Same. Same. Same. Cost includes all directly attributable costs.
Subsequent measurement Usually carried at cost less depreciation and impairment. Cost model or revaluation model. Same as Ind AS. Cost less depreciation and impairment; revaluation model generally not allowed for PPE. Ind AS/IFRS allow fair value uplift through revaluation.
Depreciation Based on useful life and residual value; component accounting less emphasized historically. Separate significant components depreciated separately. Same. Component accounting is also required where appropriate. More precise matching of cost to consumption.
Useful life review Less formalized. Reviewed at least annually. Same. Reviewed when events indicate change. Estimates are updated more actively under Ind AS/IFRS.
Residual value review Less structured. Reviewed at least annually. Same. Reviewed periodically, usually when indicators change. More frequent reassessment under Ind AS/IFRS.
Impairment Trigger-based impairment under older standards. Ind AS 36: recoverable amount approach. Same. ASC 360: recoverability test, then impairment loss if carrying value exceeds fair value. Different impairment mechanics can affect timing and size of losses.
Borrowing costs Generally capitalized when directly attributable to qualifying assets. Capitalize directly attributable borrowing costs during construction. Same. Similar capitalization for qualifying assets. Construction assets often carry interest into cost.

Exchange differences Treated under foreign currency rules. Certain FX differences on foreign currency borrowings may be capitalized in some cases. Same. FX treatment follows US GAAP foreign currency rules. Capitalization of FX is more limited and rule-based.
Revaluation surplus Not a common feature. Allowed for classes of PPE; revaluation surplus goes to OCI. Same. Generally not permitted for PPE. Major divergence between Ind AS/IFRS and US GAAP.
Disposal accounting Gain/loss recognized in P&L. Same. Same. Same. Broadly aligned at exit.

KEY INSIGHT: PPE is the area where Ind AS and IFRS are very close, while US GAAP usually stays with cost model discipline, and old AS is less detailed on component/revaluation mechanics.

SECTION L: Analyst Concerns & Answers

"Is PPE growth real or accounting?" Fear that growth is standard-driven. Largely timing: same PPE components, recognized over time now. Growth moved from X% to Y%. Same total project profit; cash flows unchanged.
"Has margin quality improved?" Window-dressing. Lifetime margins unchanged; only period allocation changed. Margin moved from X% to Y%. Component margin tables in notes.
"Are PPE component assets riskier than inventory?" Uncollectible risk. No - represent enforceable rights to payment. Right to payment disclosed.
"Are IFRS and US GAAP outcomes different?" Lack of global comparability. For these PPE components, Ind AS, IFRS and ASC 360 are identical. PPE profiles of peers.

SECTION O: Premium Intelligence Narrative

Question: "ABC Ltd's reported PPE growth changed to 22.5% after moving from AS to Ind AS/IFRS-style PPE recognition. Is this a genuine structural re-rating story or an accounting mirage?"

Average Analyst Answer: "It's mainly due to the new standard; I will look at 2-3 years of numbers and decide later."

PREMIUM Analyst Answer:

"The shift isn't just an accounting mirage; it alters the timing of earnings recognition. Componentization forces higher upfront depreciation, suppressing early-year Net Income but preventing earnings shocks later when major parts need replacement. The capitalization of major inspections smooths out maintenance expenses. The sophisticated move is to look past the depressed Year 1 earnings and recognize that cash flow remains unchanged. Instead of waiting 3 years, rebuild the normalized earnings model today, accounting for the component-level write-offs. Focus on the actual capital expenditure cash flows rather than the P&L depreciation charge."

SECTION Q: Final Intelligence Summary

The Sophistication Hierarchy

  • Level 1 (Retail): "PPE exploded and depreciation shot up. Returns on assets are plunging." → Wrong action. Misses componentization details.
  • Level 2 (Basic): "Management changed depreciation policies, earnings are artificially depressed." → Misses that underlying economics haven't changed.
  • Level 3: "Focus entirely on actual cash capital expenditure lifecycle. Componentization forces realistic depreciation that lowers early-year P&L, but cash generation is identical. Anchor valuation on sustainable cash flow." → Finds true sustainable cash flow

At a Glance: Core Differences

  • AS allows recognizing deferred tax assets for carryforward losses only with 'virtual certainty.' Ind AS, IFRS, and US GAAP use a lower 'probable' threshold.
  • IFRS and Ind AS evaluate temporary differences based on the balance sheet approach. AS traditionally used an income statement timing difference approach.
  • Under US GAAP, a valuation allowance is explicitly created against deferred tax assets if they are 'more likely than not' to go unrealized, whereas IFRS recognizes the asset net.

SECTION O: Premium Intelligence Narrative

Question: "Why can two identical companies have the same pre-tax income but different net income and ETR under the same standard?"

Average Analyst Answer: "Because one has more deferred tax assets."

PREMIUM Analyst Answer:

"The difference arises from management judgement on DTA recognition and valuation allowances. Under Ind AS/IFRS, DTA is recognised only if "probable" future profit exists; under US GAAP, a "more likely than not" threshold applies. Two identical loss-making companies can report different net income if one management believes recovery is probable and the other does not. The same applies to uncertain tax positions – IFRS uses expected value, US GAAP uses a binary "more likely than not" then measurement at the largest benefit >50% likely. The correct move is to ignore reported deferred tax adjustments for valuation purposes and instead forecast cash tax paid. For peer comps, compare cash effective tax rates, not book ETRs. The key signal is not the DTA amount, but changes in the valuation allowance – it tells you precisely what management secretly thinks about future core earnings."
